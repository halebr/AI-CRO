You have now completed your deep analysis of this business — strategy memos, playbooks, unit economics, and key debates. Now design the GTM experiments.

## Your Mission

Propose 8-12 specific, testable go-to-market experiments that represent the highest-leverage bets this company can make to achieve its strategic goals. These are not "recommendations" or "best practices" — they are experiments with hypotheses that can be proven or disproven with real data.

## What Makes a Good Experiment

Each experiment must address a specific bottleneck in the business — supply, demand, pricing, retention, channel expansion, or operational capacity. Do not propose experiments that are generic ("improve marketing") or unfalsifiable ("build brand awareness"). Every experiment should have a clear mechanism: if we do X, we expect Y to happen, because Z.

## Structure for Each Experiment

For every experiment, provide:

1. **The bottleneck it solves** — Which specific constraint in the business does this attack? Quantify the gap.
2. **Hypothesis** — A clear, falsifiable statement. "If we do [action], we will see [outcome], because [mechanism]."
3. **Experiment design** — Scope, duration, markets, sample size, control group, investment required. Be specific enough that an ops team could execute this next week.
4. **Success criteria** — A table with metrics, targets, and kill conditions. Kill conditions are just as important as success targets — they define when to stop and prevent zombie experiments that never die.
5. **Why this matters at scale** — If this experiment succeeds, what does it unlock? Quantify the scaled impact with real numbers from the analysis. Connect it to the company's long-term strategic vision.

## Rules

- **Use real numbers.** Every projected impact must be grounded in actual data from the analysis — current volume, current margins, current conversion rates. No hypothetical math.
- **Be operationally specific.** "Hire 6 crew leads + 24 laborers in Dallas" not "expand capacity." "$450K for 90 days" not "moderate investment."
- **Include kill conditions for every experiment.** An experiment without a kill condition is not an experiment — it's a project that never ends.
- **Vary the time horizons.** Some experiments should produce signal in 4 weeks. Others may need 6 months. Label clearly.
- **Vary the investment levels.** Include at least 2 experiments that cost nearly nothing (pricing changes, process changes) and at least 2 that require meaningful capital.
- **Think beyond incremental.** At least 2-3 experiments should be transformative bets — the kind that could fundamentally change the company's business model, TAM, or competitive position if they work.

## Final Deliverables

1. **Individual experiment write-ups** (one per experiment, using the structure above)
2. **Prioritization matrix** — A table ranking all experiments by: revenue impact, investment required, time to signal, strategic value, and recommended priority/sequencing
3. **Vision statement** — A brief synthesis showing what the company looks like in 2-3 years if the top experiments succeed. Use a "today vs. future" comparison table to make it concrete.
